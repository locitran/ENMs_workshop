Two widely known ENMs with numerous applications

Elastic Network Models are coarse-grained models used to study structural dynamics and conformational transitions of proteins and large biomolecules.

  • Gaussian Network Model (GNM): focuses on fluctuation magnitudes and residue couplings.
  • Anisotropic Network Model (ANM): extends this to directional motions in three dimensions.

What do ENMs study?

  • Collective, low-frequency motions that involve the whole structure.
  • Fluctuations of residues, domains, and larger structural elements.
  • Functional sites such as mechanical residues and allosteric communication paths.
  • Potential effects of pathogenic protein variants.

Each structure encodes a unique dynamics

What do ENMs do?

Proteins are not static. ENMs estimate the natural modes a protein prefers to adopt, which helps explain large-scale functional motions such as opening, closing, and breathing movements.

What does a spring actually do?

For a two-particle system with harmonic potential:

\[U(x)=\frac{1}{2}kx^2\]

At temperature T, the mean-square fluctuation becomes:

\[\langle x^2 \rangle = \frac{k_B T}{k}\]

This gives the key physical interpretation:

  • Higher temperature leads to larger fluctuations.
  • Larger spring constant leads to smaller fluctuations.
  • ENMs connect network stiffness to molecular flexibility.

Extending to 2D and 3D

In higher dimensions, fluctuations are described by covariance matrices instead of a single scalar. That is the bridge from simple springs to the matrix formulation used in GNM and ANM.
